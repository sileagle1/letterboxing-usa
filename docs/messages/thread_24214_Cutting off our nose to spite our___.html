"Tarred and feathered?" "Besmirched?" "Ensuing frenzy?" "Mob lecturing and
condemning?"

I hate to see us over react and make more of this than it is. It's really
okay to discuss responsible letterboxing here in public. By removing all the
identifying info about this box nobody should have been personally affected.
Please don't jump to judgmental conclusions and assumptions that every
discussion is a flame war. I thought all the responses so far were considerate and
well meaning.

You wouldn't believe how much stuff like this land manager complaint goes on
behind the scenes at LbNA, or how much flak the people at letterboxing.org
put up with to ensure that the website for our beloved past time is completely
free and open.

It's okay to talk about these things together as a community. Please don't
take a knee jerk negative attitude and assume we are stirring up trouble. Far
from it, we are working hard to make this work for everyone.

You probably wouldn't want to see what was in our mailbox today from a
certain National Park ranger about a plant in his area of responsibility...but
please remember that when we post to you here, it is from a friend to a friend,
and that there is a smile on the face to go with the note.

From: Lady Hydrangea Prisspott nee Hedge (lady_prisspott@yahoo.com) | Date: 2007-04-09 21:03:42 UTC
Her Ladyship would like to present a true tale of her experiences at
the Manor that seems an apt parable for the state of letterboxing.

Her Ladyship, as a delicate young nymph, moved into the old stone
country estate known as Prisspott Manor. Located in the rolling
pastures and wooded glens of the Blue Ridge foothills, Prisspott Manor
was a great gray lady of native limestone and drafty eaves. Like any
great lady Prisspott Manor was imposing but showing it's age around
the edges; just as one might see the occasional wisp of hair that is
no longer held as tightly in place in the coif of a mature lady so the
manor had the occasional loose board at the eaves.

For several years it was an idyllic existence, ensconced as her
Ladyship was, in in the warm and comforting glow of the Manor's
hearth; a West Virginian Vesta, safe from the small fauna that shared
the provincial landscape. How fortunate her Ladyship thought herself
to be free of the annoyance of the small rodents whith which one would
expect to have a chance encounter in such a country setting. For some
time her Ladyship was untroubled by any scurrying in the walls or
nibbling on the grain stores for unbeknownst to her the Manor was
inhabited by a stealthy tenant who had the good sense to remain silent
and never to show it's self to the Lady of the manor.

With time comes complacency and one day her Ladyship discovered the
silent sentry that kept the vermin at bay; a large and black snake,
failing to make sufficient haste, slithered right up the side of the
manor clinging in the chinks between the stones disappearing into the
attic through a loose board in the eaves. Her Ladyship paused for
reflection unhampered by panic through her familiarity with the habits
and temperament of the many creatures of the surrounding field and
dale and realized that her silent guest had for years remained both
invisible and diligent. Thus an arrangement developed that brought her
Ladyship a blissful vermin-free contentment. The guest was allowed to
come and go at will enjoying the blessing of free will and liberty
that sweetens our miserable time on earth. If there were to be guests
in the Manors dark recesses, and there surely would be, then her
Ladyship reasoned that this quiet and unassuming guest was preferred.

For years never a trace of this visitor was left and for a time no
trace would be seen but, as will happen, this easy coexistence lead
the visitor to become more and more brazen and gradually to invite
guest of his own. At first her Ladyship merely had to show her
displeasure at the encounter and for a time they would again forget
each other's existence. While the guest had been tolerated as long as
it left to trace it was not welcome showing it's self with increasing
frequency and her Ladyship had come to believe that it was not always
the same individual.

Soon these guests became recalcitrant to her Ladyships efforts to
encourage them to find other lodgings and the guest began expressing
their displeasure to her Ladyship with increasing venom, often raring
up and hissing their protest directly at her Ladyship. Such
unassailable confidence began to invite her Ladyship's retribution.

A thorough inspection of the Manor reveled that the silent tenant had
abused her Ladyship's indulgence and had created a large colony
throughout the upper reaches of the Manor. Eviction proceedings were
begun at once. Years of tolerance lead the creatures to develop a
sense of entitlement and they resisted her Ladyship's demand to vacate
most vehemently but divine right was on her Ladyship's side and such
unabashed presumptuousness, such self-righteous resistance, the
unfounded and ill-reasoned sense of entitlement were given short
shrift and Saint Patrick was visited upon their prideful souls. The
guest, and all his kind, were dispatched to meet the maker to whom
their cries of repentance came too late. For their sins of pride and
greed they were punished and their sense of entitlement slipped away
as easily as their soul slipped the bounds of the mortal coil.

But that wasn't the end. With the ill-mannered guests gone the mice
arrived, even more loutish and unwelcome a guest by far. Now it was
only the mice that were happy, her Ladyship was plagued by their
scurrying and the damage done by their incessant gnawing and stealing
of food and the snakes, well, the snakes were feeling no emotion
anymore. This unhappy turn of events lead to a fortification of the
manor against any guests, the eaves were boarded up, holes plugged and
cracks filled until her Ladyship was hermetically sealed inside the
manor.

Last to arrive was an innocent looking feline with a wild streak who
was plucked from a care-free, libertine life and imprisoned in the
manor. There it lead a predictable existence and performed it's sentry
duty against any unwanted vermin well but it still did not enjoy the
blessings of liberty that her Ladyship's first guest was granted lest
it entice more of it's kind and her Ladyship found herself the uneasy
jailer of this otherwise innocent animal who surely longed to roam
free as it had in youth but now suffered the misery of dull routine
with only the occasional unwanted guest upon which to exercise it's
frustration.

At least one Oregon State Park (Banks-Vernonia Trail) has pulled
geocaches. This same park had a whole series of letterboxes go

minimize the chance that the powers to be decide they don't want
letterboxing in their parks.
