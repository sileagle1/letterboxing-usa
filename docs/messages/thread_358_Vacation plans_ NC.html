Hi Lynn, welcome. There may actually, technically be a letterbox
in NC although it is listed under Tennesse. Its called Max Patch
near Max Patch Mountain, NC I believe. The clue can be found at
http://www.pclink.com/elf/maxpatch.htm
This is in Western NC/Eastern TN. This clue is a bit different as it
starts at another letterbox in Sewanee, TN. That clue can be found at
http:http://www.pclink.com/elf/sewanee.htm You will see that the clues
are linked together. If you want to see an overview of all U.S.
letterboxes go to http://www.pclink.com/elf/
and scroll down to the maps, then on the right-hand U.S. map click on
Tennessee, there's one in MD also which isn't too far away. I don't
know where you are travelling, but this map (by clicking on different
places) will reveal to you where the ones are that you may be getting
near to in your travels.

As for how to plant boxes my recommendation is to get a good (well-
sealing) container. If you want I will send a few to you for free.
Just send me your snail mail address to mailto:elf@pclink.com and
ask for some containers. I also have one sample log book left. The
log book is moisture proof. If you are going to plant a box in a place
where you or somebody else may not be able to inspect it for a long
time I would highly recommend the moisture proof log book and a
container that seals well. You will also need some stamps. Many
people on this list (perhaps 1/2 to 2/3) carve their own stamps from
rubber erasers. If you are interested in doing that just ask and
I'm sure somebody will give you some pointers. I carved my own first
stamp just last week. It consisted of 14 small letters. It didn't
turn out exactly the way I would have liked but I'm very proud of it.
You can also have a stamp made from your own drawing or some clipart
and you can also purchase them ready made.

Well there's lots more to know. Ask questions. Poeple are very
helpful. Also, visit the Letterboxing USA site at
http://www.pclink.com/elf
and check out Getting Started and About. That's where the clues are
also.

Good Luck. Don't be a stranger.

Dan Servatius

Do to a rare birth defect, I was born with stamp pad ink in my veins, instead
of blood. Fortunately it was red, so no one notices. Actually, I have been
MAD about stamping for about fourteen years now. It all started when I
stumbled upon a rare find at a used book sale: "The Rubber Stamp Album" by
Joni K. Miller and Lowry Thompson.

Soon I was hooked... carving my own stamps, buying them from mail-order
catalogs, and finding them at flea markets. I must say, I was well ahead of
my time... back then I could hardly find them in retail stores. Now it seems
every craft, book, card, and toy store carries a nice line of stamps.

I use them any way I can. I love Mail Art. My profusely decorated letters
have undoubtedly caused many a disgruntled postal employee to "lose it"!! Who
knows how many ghosts haunt me as a result. I am a public menace in more ways
than one... my stamps have been know to show up in the oddest public places,
from waiting room magazines to bulletin boards to (heaven forbid) restroom
walls. On occasion I have even been known to protest certain products by
stamping activist messages on the boxes as they sat on the shelf in a store.
My name has been Der Mad Stamper for nearly a decade and a half, and I have
definetely lived up to it!!!

I make maps with stamps, as well as cards, calendars, paperless wallpaper
designs, wrapping paper, and works of art (or MADsterpieces, as I prefer to
call them).

Another fun activity that I enjoy is writing letters to people in the form of
a REBUS. A rebus is a puzzle where many of the words in a message are
replaced with pictures. Sometimes I use stamps with math symbols to create an
elaborate formula to define the words, for example, the word STAMPER might be
replaced with a formula that has a stamp of a STAR minus AR plus a stamped pic
of a LAMP minus L plus a stamp of a lady with the word "mom" (to represent the
word MOTHER) minus a picture of a MOTH. Get it?

To be quite honest, I had been so busy in very recent years that I seldom made
time for stamping, except on special occasions, like birthdays, valentines,
etc. Then, I discovered the Smithsonian article about letterboxing in
(ironically enough) a waiting room. I could almost hear the fuses blowing in
my head as I read the article! I rushed home, logged on to the Net, typed the
word LETTERBOXING into a search engine, and before long found myself
conversing by email with Erik Davis in VT and Dan Servatius in MN. A
letterboxing fanatic was born!!!

Now that you know more about me than my MOTH plus ER, perhaps you will
consider marrying me. I will clean all 5000 of your stamps regularly and
write love notes to you every day (if you can figure out what they say).
There's only one catch. The prenups must state that if we should break up,
you get the cars, the bikes, the house and all the money... but I get all the
rubber stamps!!!

Madly yours,
Stampie
