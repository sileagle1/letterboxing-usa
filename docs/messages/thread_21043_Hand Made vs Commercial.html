MissMoon wrote:
<< As a little side note, a lot of the stamps I found in the box I sent to

Me, too!
And I am grateful for every single stamp in our logbook, commercial or
hand-carved! When teen-aged son CactusLicker & I signed up for MMM, we still
had no idea what we were doing, and figured this was a way to become part of
a bigger community.
Well, it far exceeded our expectations!
I can't tell you how many times we've opened our Sphinx logbook and
delighted in the notes folks wrote - we especially got a kick out of people
telling us whether or not they guessed the riddle!
Personally, I could care less whether the stamp is hand-carved. I enjoy
seeing hand-carved images (and would give my right pinkie to be able to
carve like ScoutDogs!) but there are so many other aspects to enjoy - the
hike, the view, the company, the notes, the emails from great finders.

Ruby Tuesday
(A woefully inept carver, but totally devoted boxer)

We have been letterboxing for just over a year also. Like many
others We too used a commercial stamp. It fit our needs. It got us
out and boxing. Just last month we were able to find something that
was good enough to carve for our signature stamp. When we find a box
we are happy that someone took the time to plant that box, not look
in the box and have something about what type of stamp is in it. New
Stamp, Old Stamp, Fresh Cut, Old and Worn Out, Elaborate Commercial,
or just a Few Lines scratched on an old eraser. That stamp meant
something to that person. They wanted to share that with someone
else. Feel sorry for them, not us. We are happy they joined the
hobby that we enjoy so much.

Re: [LbNA] Hand Made vs Commercial

From: Judy B (sowbiz@yahoo.com) | Date: 2004-12-02 15:09:21 UTC

Let me add that MizScarlet is also a stamp carver extraodinaire -- so
if you got it flaunt it.

But for me I don't mind the personal stamps being commercial - it is
the box stamps I prefer to be hand carved or at least commercially
amended so no two are alike. Why can't folks nick chip or reglue to
make it individual.

So no one has mentioned the quasi-commercial version. For the Red
Hat Box'rs I made a team stamp from a kit. You make a mold using a
xerox image and then pour in the rubber goop and it makes a stamp.
It also takes a lot of time but can have some very small detail -
like text and dots. And when you lose the stamp, you pour more goop
and make another (assuming you haven't lost the mold). OR you can
make multiples for all members of the team (yeah yeah yeah, I'll get
to it next year.) Here is the product:
http://www.artisanschoice.com/tellmeallaboutstamps1.html

In Dartmoor, some folks would do original art drawings, cluster as
many as possible on an 8x10 sheet and then send it off for commercial
production. Cut the rubber apart and make many stamps. Again there
is an element of the original, unique, artsy fartsy.

From: cahillymom (acahilly@prodigy.net) | Date: 2004-12-07 02:31:05 UTC

Ok, now that this has become more of a story-telling thread, I'll
bite the bait:
I have a commercial stamp. I didn't buy it. I won it. Years and
years ago I got lots of things for my kids from the Toys to Grow On
Catalog. They ran a contest in one issue where there was a little
cartoon mouse hiding around in and among the toys. If you could
correctly count his appearances and entered the number in a box on
the order form, you got a free stamp of the mouse. So I did, and I
did. In the following Christmas catalog I got a stamp of the same
mouse dressed as a jester the same way. I threw them both in the box
with my serious home-schooling stamps (coins, blank clock-face...)
and forgot about them.
Then came Smithsonian, then hubby discovered geocaching, and finally
the day when he sent me the LbNA link. I solved a mystery clue,
screamed "I KNOW where it is!!!" and the next day grabbed my mouse,
stopped at Staples for ink (black) and book (lined journal) and was
off and running.
So if you ever see my jaunty little mouse, and notice the
resemblance to Bilbo without his pocket-handkerchief or Mole
abandoning the whitewash, you'll see why I've kept him.
(now, what do I do with the jester and the serious stamps?...)
