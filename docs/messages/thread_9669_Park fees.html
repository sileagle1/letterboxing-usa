State Park Fees--an explanation.
A couple of years ago there was a statewide initiative that the
citizens of Washington State voted on to eliminate excessive car
tab fees. The initiative passed and every vehicle in the state
(theoretically) was from then on charged a flat rate of $30 per
year for licensing. Local governments then began to charge
various fees to suppliment the moneys they lost but overall the
$30 license tabs stuck. This caused a huge dent in the state
coffers. Liberal politicians and beaurocrats began whining
about the lost revenue and how this would destroy much
progress in fighting hunger, poor health conditions, school
lunches, etc. The politicians were forced to begin to cut
spending and one of the first things they did was cut funding to
the state parks system as a punishment of sorts to the voters for
passing the initiative. Many parks were closed altogether and
many more were slated for seasonal closures. Use fees began
to be implemented to help pay for maintenance and salaries of
skeleton staff.
Since the original initiative passed, the state courts overturned
the initiative calling it unconstitutional. Smart legislators heard
the people speak at the polls and quickly passed legislation in
the state house and senate to cut the tab fees to the $30 limit.
Other initiatives have been run and more progress has been
made from a consumers standpoint on cutting onerous taxes at
the state level. But the politicians and beauraucrats just keep
whining. We now have been informed (no public vote was
offered) by the beauraucrats in the state parks department that
there must be these expensive use fees in order to save the
parks system.

Although I dont agree with the methods that the Washington
State Parks system used to arrive at this move, and I certainly
dont agree with the state courts on the ruling that the car tab
initiative is unconstitutional, I do believe in use fees. The people
who use the parks should support the parks. Unfortunately, this
will limit many peoples access or at least discourage it. It is a
shame that it had to come to this. My family will purchase an
annual permit to access all the parks in the state. We will
probably do the same for Oregon Parks as there are many
letterboxes within a days drive of us here in Olympia, WA. Many
other people who may be interested in letterboxing may not be
able to afford this however, and that is just really a shame.

I call on all letterboxers in the Washington and Oregon areas
(and other states too) to continue to place new boxes in city
parks and other municipal areas that do not have access fees in
order to keep the sport affordable to new participants. Dont stop
placing boxes in the state parks or other areas requiring access
or entry or parking fees but be considerate of those folks who
cannot afford the $50 or $70 per year or $5 per day fees.
Sometines it is well worth the fee to get to a great box or better
yet a great location box or not. But still, we must be considerate.

Nailhead

Well said Nailhead--I, too, will get an annual state park pass, I
just find it WAY steep compared to other states where I've purchased
annual passes in my lifetime (TX, AZ, CA, NPS)--it's the same amount
as a National Park Pass which gets you into spectacular parks around
the entire country!!! This one really bites the pocketbook BIG
TIME! There is an incredible little park a few miles down the road
from my house with a gorgeous beach along the Sound with a few
possible letterbox hiding spots, I've avoided placing a box there
simply because it has a $3 parking fee! Long story but I figured my
outrageous property taxes already covered local park maintenance--
guess not--so, in a way, I've sort of been conducting a personal
boycott of the little park.

As for those pesky car tabs, I'm still holding my breath for the $30
tabs. grumble, grumble... ;) Last time I registered a car a few
months back, it exceeded $100!

Yes, I reiterate what Nailhead says regarding placing boxes in all
different venues around this beautiful country in an effort to keep
this wonderful sport open to folks from all different financial
backgrounds/situations! The low cost factor is really a major
attraction of this sport (besides the adventure aspect of it). I
also dabble in scuba diving, skiing, swimming & triathlons--
letterboxing has been, by far, the least expensive, the most
rewarding and something I can do with the entire family (including
the little ones)!!!

Thanks for such thoughtful comments!

From: Mary Ellen Martel (memlili54@yahoo.com) | Date: 2002-12-15 05:25:12 UTC-08:00

I must try to share a bit of perspective from someone who lives surrounded by Acadia National Park.  Those of us who are fortunate to have parks in our backyards also have to share them with those who are not so fortunate.  As more areas are eaten up by development, the parks become even more popular.  Acadia has the highest ratio of visitors per square mile of any National Park in the country.  The number of visitors coming to our parks every year is huge.  This has a tremendous impact on the natural setting and the infrastructure.  Add to this the staffing required to adequately oversee these areas and there is not adequate funding from taxation to take care of these issues.  And we won't even consider the costs of sending out the search and resuce team every time someone decides they don't want to follow the trail, would rather make their own and fall down the side of a mountain.  The point is,  if we want to have these areas preserved for our enjoyment and that of our children and grandchildren, we have to help provide financial support.  Yes,  I swallow when I pay a park fee (especially here on the island - afterall, this is MY home), but most day fees are not much more than I would pay for a 2-hour video, which would not be nearly as beneficial for my body, spirit and soul.  Maybe annual park passes should be appearing on people's holiday gift lists!
