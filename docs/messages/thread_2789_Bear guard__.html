From: Kimberly Mako (MartianShark@msn.com) | Date: 2001-07-11 16:50:48 UTC-04:00
 
 Before you guys go all gung ho on this bear guard, maybe you should pick up a back issue of Backpacker magazine, or hit up their website.  They did a review on it a few months ago, and found that none of it works to deter bears, all it actually did was make them angry before they attacked.  And think of this as well.  An angry bear is charging at you, and you're thinking enough to grab the bear spray, did you check to see if you're downwind? So now you can't see, and there's still that bear?  I've never had a bear attack me, so I'm not an authority.  I have woken up while backpacking and one was sniffing my tent.  I was right up against the mesh lining, so all I saw was a bear nose in front of my face.  I just waiting and it left, I didn't have anything it wanted.  I've seen plenty of bears while hiking, and have always had them run the other way.  I've even had them walk through my camp and stare up at my food hanging from a tree, while I was sitting there.  I've always left them alone and they go away.  I've heard that if they chase you you've done something to their young or you have something that smells fantastic on you.  If it's the first, then you deserve it, if it's the latter, give the food to them!  Knowledge will help you more than bear spray.  I'm not saying there is one specific thing that you should do, because all cases are not the same.  But I just think there's something else you should depend on, not the spray.  Save that for the humans :)

Actually if you are referring to their Sept 2000 article that is
_not_ what it says. I originally learned about bear spray from an
earlier backpacker article. In part the Sept 2000 article says:

****
Dr. Herrero, a noted researcher of bear behavior and attacks, along
with Andrew Higgins, a university colleague, examined 66 field cases
in which various brands of spray were used on black and grizzly bears
that displayed behavior ranging from overly curious to actively
aggressive toward humans. They concluded that, "while we don't know
how these encounters would have ended in the absence of spray, the
use...appears to have prevented injury in most cases," Dr. Herrero
says.
****

As they note in the article it is a last resort when you really feel
you are in danger of being mauled, not just for curious bears. My
point is if you are going to carry a weapon (and spray is a weapon
though not lethal) it is best to carry the most powerful available.
Hopefully you won't ever need it for a bear, much more likely a
human. If it came down to it and I was charged by an aggressive bear
(or boar, gator, human etc..) I would rather have the most powerful
spray available than only my hands. No false security for me, just a
last resort if it came to that.

From: Jay Chamberlain (ae4mk@1bigred.com) | Date: 2001-07-12 10:42:27 UTC-04:00
This may sound strange, but as a Federal law enforcement officer, in order to be "qualified" to carry and use "pepper spray"  we must be sprayed with it. I am so glad they don't use the same premises about carrying my issued handgun.
 
The reason behind this training is to know just how you will react when coming in contact with the stuff. 
 
As a human voice of experience, it is not fun stuff to encounter and the effects stays with you for a long time (hours).
 
My point is, ANY pepper spray or chemical agent must be handled with caution. It is very indiscriminant. It is just as easy to be on the receiving end of a dose intended for an animal if you are not aware of whether it has a stream or a mist and how far it will carry and take into account the wind. As a rule, unless you are in a VERY active bear area, and have had some kind of "training" with the spray, I wouldn't carry it. I have had two, day time eastern Black bear encounters while hiking and back packing. In both, the bears were in a very big hurry to get away from me. I also take the required precaution while backpacking to hang a bear bag at night, at least 100 - 150 feet from my tent, for ALL food items including things like cooking pots and pans, Chap Stick, tooth past, cloths items with spilled food on it. ANYTHING that a four legged friend might think smells like food. Also I cook away from my sleeping area and place my backpack 20-30 feet away from the tent too.
 
I would also check with your local hiking and outdoor adventure, equipment store. The guys who know their local area and get their recommendations....
 
My two cents.....
