From: Jeremy Disch (jdisch@emerald.tufts.edu) | Date: 2001-05-07 19:57:54 UTC-04:00
Not to ruffle any feathers but...

I happen to agree with Randy. I feel a bit shakky about seeing stamp
images scanned onto finder's websites.

I don't mind if authors do it, I am sure they would love to show off
their handywork. But I think it will sometimes spoil the surprise of a
good stamp if I see it before I offically find it.

In fact there are a few stamps I have spent hours carving (especially the
?? ones) and I think it would be disappointing to see it displayed for the
world to see. I would like to keep them mysteries as they were intended.

I however like the idea of keeping an online log of where I have
been, and just simply listing a table of dates and letterboxes should be
adequate enough for my tastes.

Jeremy Disch

From: Judi (Judi@SpaceFamily.net) | Date: 2001-05-08 10:46:18 UTC-04:00
Hi Keith
    One of the things that is most fun about letterboxing to me is the discovery of the image on the stamp when I finally find the box. If the images are scanned and published, it takes away from the surprise. One might argue that the very purpose of the rubber stamp medium is to duplicate an image, just as it is the purpose of scanning technology. Of course, I've already seen the images of the boxes that I have placed, and I've seen images of the boxes that other hunters have found when they show me their log books, but having the image available to view any time reduces its uniqueness.
           
            Space Traveler P13F25
