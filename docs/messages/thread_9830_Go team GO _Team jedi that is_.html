From: Randy Hall (randy@mapsurfer.com) | Date: 2002-12-26 17:14:59 UTC-05:00

> [Jeremy] is basically the one who derived geocaching from letterboxing

Just for the record and not that it matters, but this is untrue. Dave
Ulmer invented geocaching (it was called gps stash hunt in those days)
independent of letterboxing as a reaction to SA being turned off. Matt
Strom (and someone else other than Jeremy) invented the term
"geocaching"; and by the time geocaching.com was online, the activity
already had its basic form [...]. I could go on, but the history is
public or at least it used to be if it isn't now.

As for non-straighforward clues, there are tons of them (but perhaps not
in percentage terms). Sometimes they find you ... or they are invisible
to those who don't have what it takes (or the interest/time/resources) to
solve them anyway, so this arrangement tends to work out. In any case,
I claim the clues I've written are generally non-straightforward for the
most part, and tend to offer a large diversity of challenges:
http://www.mapsurfer.com/boxes/index.html ... but you can do what you want,
and people will ignore you if they don't like it. If your question
was ... can I do non-straightforward clues and expect people to look for
them ... that is a different kettle of fish altogether, and something to
consider if you care about people looking for them ...

As for your other question on the length of the hikes, I tend to agree that
the hikes are too short for east coast letterboxes in general. Before
anyone flames me, just my opinion :-) Aside from Jay Drew's "Feather"
series in Rhode Island, I can't remember a hike of more than 2 or 3 miles,
and that was to find 4 boxes, so the average may have been in that range.
Most hikes tend to be less than a mile, so by all means, throw some 20
milers at us ... with impossible clues to boot ;-)

Cheers
