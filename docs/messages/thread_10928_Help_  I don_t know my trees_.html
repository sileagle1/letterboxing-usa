Try these:

http://www.oplin.lib.oh.us/products/tree/
http://www.arborday.org/trees/treeID.html

I carry a copy of the "National Audubon Society Field Guide to the
Mid-Atlantic States" in my pack. They publish them for various regions of
the US (Southeastern, Southwestern, Rocky Mountain, Pacific Northwest, New
England). I like this publication because it's all-inclusive -- they cover
trees, plants, birds, animals with descriptions and color photos in one
volume. Audubon does publish individual guides to trees, birds, rocks and
minerals, seashells, etc., but it's a lot easier to carry one book than
several! They're $20 each -- but $13.97 at Amazon.com (or you can get some
of them for $9.99 used).

Make sure to search for "National Audubon Society Field Guide to ...",
since there are lots of Audubon field guides in and out of print.

From: acahilly@prodigy.net <acahilly@prodigy.net> (acahilly@prodigy.net) | Date: 2003-02-26 12:34:45 UTC
Our vote for "Trees of the Eastern and Central United States and
Canada" by Willam M. Harlow, published as part of the Dover field
guide series. I'm choosing it over others because that's what Wayne
uses when he teaches the Tree I.D. course at the New York Botanical
Garden. The key being the key. Any guide is good for answering "what
does an oak look like?" but when you have an unidentified tree in
front of you, you want a "dichotomous" (sp?) key. It gives you a
series of "is it like this or that?" (choice of only two answers)
questions about leaves, bark, fruits, etc., with each answer leading
you to the next question. When you answer the final question, the
answer leads you to the description page of the tree in question.
The home-school mom in me has to mention that this is great for
building observation skills. You may need to bring a small hand lens
along to check for things like fine hairs on leaves.
